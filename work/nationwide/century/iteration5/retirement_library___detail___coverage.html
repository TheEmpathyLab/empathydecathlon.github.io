Finally understand the jargon and learn about insurance options 

Coverage

Most people are covered for healthcare through their employer. COBRA allows you to continue with the coverage provided by your employer for up to 18 months after you have left, but you will likely need to pay the premiums yourself. 


For individuals not covered by their employers, there is public marketplace coverage provided through the Affordable Care Act. Coverage under the ACA varies by state and region, but all offer four levels of coverage (Bronze, Silver, Gold, and Platinum). Learn more here. A private marketplace is also available through private health insurers. Here you will find the greatest variety of coverage and insurance options, but it is also more expensive than public marketplace coverage.


Finally, Medicare and Medicaid are government-provided coverage options. Medicare is coverage for individuals who are above full retirement age (currently 67 for those born after 1960). Medicaid is coverage low income individuals and families.


What are the different types of insurance available to retirees?

Your most predictable healthcare expense is your premium, or the monthly cost of your healthcare coverage. However, you will also likely need to pay additional out-of-pocket expenses each year, including coinsurance, copayments, and deductibles. Healthcare costs vary greatly from person to person and are largely dependent on your health and coverage. Healthcare plans limit your total out of pocket expenses to a maximum out-of-pocket amount, so the most you can pay in a given year is your maximum out-of-pocket amount plus your total annual premium.

How much does healthcare cost?


The more money you pay in monthly premium, the less you will need to pay in out-of-pocket expenses. In addition to your premium, you share the cost of your healthcare with your provider in several ways - through a deductible, copayment, or coinsurance.


A deductible is an amount that you pay for a given service before your insurance begins to pay. A copayment is a fixed amount that you pay for a particular medical service. Copays are commonly used with prescription drugs and standard visits to your doctor. Coinsurance is a variable amount that you pay for a particular medical service. For example, coinsurance may cover 80% of the cost of a specific procedure, leaving you to cover the remaining 20%.

How do out-of-pocket expenses work?


Deductible - the amount you pay before your insurance begins to cover your healthcare expenses.

Terminology

Coinsurance - an variable amount that you pay for a specific medical service. For example, coinsurance may cover 80% of the cost of a specific procedure, leaving you to cover the remaining 20%. 

Copayment - a fixed amount that you pay for a specific medical service. Commonly used for prescription medications.

Premium - the monthly fee you pay for your healthcare coverage.

Provider network - a set of healthcare providers that are part of your health insurance plan. 

Maximum out-of-pocket amount - the maximum amount that you could pay out-of-pocket under your health plan.

High deductible health plan (HDHP) – A high deductible health plan is a health insurance policy that features higher deductibles and lower premiums than traditional insurance plans. HDHPs can be combined with a health savings account or a health reimbursement arrangement that allows for payment of qualified out-of-pocket medical expenses on a pre-tax basis. Covers expenses either before or during retirement.
